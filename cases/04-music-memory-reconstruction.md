# Record of Purpose

This interaction explores how humans often do not preserve memories as complete retrievable datasets.

Instead, memory may survive as fragmented combinations of:

- emotional atmosphere
- visual tone
- movement impression
- rhythm fragments
- sensory association
- identity-state linkage
- temporal emotional imprint

The user attempted to reconstruct a forgotten Japanese drama song from approximately 17 years ago using:
- humming fragments
- symbolic rhythm notation
- piano single-note reconstruction
- MV atmosphere description
- emotional association

Despite:
- audio samples,
- reconstructed melody fragments,
- rhythmic structure,
- MV visual clues,
- estimated timeframe,
- language category,
- and genre narrowing,

the AI system remained unable to confidently identify the song.

---

# Observed Human Memory Characteristics

This case demonstrates that human memory is often:

- non-linear
- emotionally indexed
- atmosphere-linked
- sensory-associated
- identity-state dependent
- incomplete yet emotionally persistent

The remembered object was not:
- the song title,
- lyrics,
- artist,
- or precise melody.

Instead, the preserved memory consisted of:
- a grey/black/white MV atmosphere,
- female vocal tone,
- contemporary dance imagery,
- opening melodic contour,
- emotional resonance from a past life period.

The “memory object” therefore existed more as:

# emotional reconstruction

than factual retrieval.

---

# AI Limitation Signals Observed

## 1️⃣ Fragmented Melody Matching Difficulty

Current AI music identification systems heavily depend on:
- accurate pitch,
- timing consistency,
- lyrics,
- or waveform similarity.

Human reconstructed humming often contains:
- pitch drift,
- tempo instability,
- missing intervals,
- emotional phrasing distortions,
- incomplete sections.

This creates large ambiguity gaps.

---

## 2️⃣ Weak Atmospheric Memory Interpretation

The AI struggled to interpret:
- “grey MV feeling”
- “modern ballet atmosphere”
- “2000s emotional tone”
- “urban loneliness aesthetic”

because these are:

# interpretive emotional abstractions

not objective metadata.

---

## 3️⃣ Poor Identity-State Retrieval Modeling

Humans may remember:
- who they were,
- where they were emotionally,
- what life phase they were in,

more strongly than the actual content itself.

The memory retrieval process was therefore:

# self-state reconstruction

not merely song retrieval.

Current AI systems have limited longitudinal emotional-state reconstruction capability.

---

## 4️⃣ Cross-Sensory Reconstruction Weakness

Even with:
- hearing,
- rhythm,
- piano notes,
- vocal contour,

reconstruction remained difficult.

This suggests smell-based memory retrieval would be vastly harder.

Human smell memory is deeply linked to:
- emotion,
- survival,
- childhood encoding,
- environmental atmosphere,
- relational memory,
- subconscious association.

Current AI systems possess extremely weak:
- olfactory abstraction models,
- emotional scent mapping,
- cross-sensory identity linkage.
